2:title: "Subnet 77: Liquidity"
3:summary:
4:  "Liquidity is Bittensor Subnet 77, a subnet where token-holder votes steer liquidity incentives
5:  and miners compete by supplying liquidity to selected decentralized-exchange pools."
6:category: "Subnets"
7:tags: ["Subnets"]

9:
10:Liquidity is Bittensor Subnet 77, a subnet for directing incentives toward decentralized-exchange
11:liquidity. Its public codebase describes a liquidity mining system for the Bittensor ecosystem in
12:which token holders vote on pools and validators use those votes to help set miner weights.
13:
14:The practical idea is simple: projects and communities often need deeper liquidity so their tokens
15:can be traded with less friction. Subnet 77 turns that need into a Bittensor reward market by
16:letting liquidity providers compete for subnet incentives while token-holder voting helps decide
17:which pools matter most.
18:
19:## What Liquidity Rewards
20:
21:Subnet 77 rewards supplied liquidity rather than model output, generated content, or compute
22:benchmarks. Miners participate by providing liquidity to the pools favored by the subnet's voting
23:process. The more useful a miner's liquidity is to the selected pools, the stronger the basis for
24:rewarding that miner.
25:
26:This makes Liquidity a DeFi-oriented subnet. It is concerned with market depth and liquidity
27:allocation, not with training an AI model directly. The subnet still uses the Bittensor incentive
28:layer, but the work being measured happens in external liquidity pools.
29:
30:## Voting and Weighting
31:
32:The subnet introduces a voting layer so token holders can express which pools should receive
33:attention. Those votes guide the system toward pools that the community wants to support, instead of
34:leaving liquidity incentives fixed by a single static list.
35:
36:Validators translate the voting and liquidity information into subnet weights. At a high level, they
37:look at which pools have been selected, how liquidity is distributed across those pools, and which
38:miners are responsible for that liquidity. Those weights then flow through
39:[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) to determine how rewards are
40:allocated on the subnet.
41:
42:## Vote and Liquidity Mapping Context
43:
44:The
45:[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md)
46:describes three separate pieces that have to line up before miner rewards make sense: token-holder
47:votes, liquidity positions, and Bittensor miner identities. Token holders vote on which pools should
48:receive weight, miners provide liquidity to those pools, and validators fetch calculated weights
49:that connect the two.
50:
51:That mapping is important because the useful work happens outside the Bittensor chain. A miner's
52:liquidity position is associated with an EVM address, while subnet rewards are assigned to a
53:Bittensor hotkey. The README describes a registration step that links those identities, giving the
54:weighting system a way to credit the miner responsible for a liquidity position.
55:
56:The same source also describes pool voting as weighted rather than purely one-person-one-vote. Votes
57:express which liquidity pools token holders want to support, while miner positions determine who is
58:actually supplying liquidity inside those selected pools. For readers, the subnet is therefore not
59:rewarding liquidity in isolation and not rewarding votes alone; it is combining both signals into a
60:liquidity-incentive market.
61:
62:That combination also keeps the article's DeFi framing precise. Pool demand can change as token
63:holders update preferences, while miner credit depends on whether capital is actually present in the
64:relevant pools. The reward target is useful, attributable liquidity for pools the mechanism has
65:selected.
66:
67:This helps explain why validators are described as a measurement layer. Their role is not to invent
68:the preferred pools manually. They follow the calculated weight result from the vote and liquidity
69:system, then submit subnet weights so emissions track the pools and miner positions the mechanism is
70:meant to incentivize.
71:
72:References:
73:[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
74:[Subnet 77 repository](https://github.com/creativebuilds/sn77)
75:
76:## Miner and Validator Roles
77:
78:Miners are liquidity providers. Their job is to place capital where the subnet is directing
79:liquidity demand, then keep enough useful liquidity in those pools to remain competitive.
80:
81:Validators are the measurement and weighting layer. They follow the subnet's voting result, observe
82:the liquidity supplied by miners, and set weights that flow through
83:[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) into emissions each tempo, so
84:rewards track the pools and positions the subnet is meant to incentivize. Operators should treat the
85:Subnet 77 repository as the source of truth for current implementation details.
86:
87:## On-Chain Identity
88:
89:Live SN77 subnet data is available on [TaoStats](https://taostats.io/subnets/77), which identifies
90:Subnet 77 as Liquidity. The live Finney identity for netuid 77 registers the subnet name as
91:Liquidity, with the description "Supply liquidity on external chains via uniswap, incentivize any
92:project." The registered project website is [sn77.xyz](https://sn77.xyz) and the GitHub repository
93:is [creativebuilds/sn77](https://github.com/creativebuilds/sn77), whose README describes the voting,
94:liquidity, and validator-weighting model used by the subnet.
95:
96:## Development Stage Context
97:
98:The
99:[Introduction to Bittensor](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/introduction#subnet-development)
100:describes subnet development as moving from localnet to testnet and then mainnet. For Subnet 77,
101:that sequence applies to the standard Bittensor lifecycle: localnet for isolated development,
102:testnet for shared non-production testing, and mainnet for live operation with real emissions.
103:
104:On mainnet, Subnet 77 is registered as the live production subnet at netuid 77. The
105:[Bittensor Networks](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/concepts/bittensor-networks) reference
106:separates mainnet, testnet, and localnet. Participation examples or emission outcomes from one
107:environment should not be read as representing production subnet performance in another environment.
108:
109:## Distinction from Yuma Consensus
110:
111:Liquidity on netuid 77 is a DeFi liquidity-mining subnet where token holders vote on decentralized-
112:exchange pools and miners supply liquidity that validators map to Bittensor hotkeys. That
113:vote-to-liquidity vocabulary names how SN77 measures pool-directed incentives on netuid 77, not the
114:on-chain step that turns validator weight submissions into emission shares each tempo
115:([creativebuilds/sn77](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
116:[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus)).
117:
118:Yuma Consensus is the on-chain mechanism that runs at the epoch boundary on the selected netuid. It
119:reads the weight matrix from eligible validators, applies clipping and bonding, and converts the
120:result into miner incentives and validator dividend shares
121:([Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus),
122:[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).
123:
124:Earlier sections link EVM liquidity positions to registered miner identities through a mapping step.
125:Validators combine weighted pool votes with measured liquidity distribution before setting weights.
126:Yuma aggregates those submitted weight signals rather than trading on external DEX pools itself
127:([Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
128:[Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets)).
129:
130:Community pool voting therefore sits upstream of settlement. Yuma still credits emissions from
131:included validator weights on netuid 77 once the tempo closes
132:([creativebuilds/sn77](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
133:[Emission](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/emissions)).
134:
135:Readers should treat Liquidity as the vote-steered DEX liquidity protocol on netuid 77, and Yuma
136:Consensus as the recurring mechanism that allocates rewards from validator weights each tempo.
137:
138:- **Subnet 77 (Liquidity)** — token-holder pool voting and liquidity-mining subnet.
139:- **Yuma Consensus** — epoch-boundary settlement from validator weights to emission shares on a task
140:  subnet
141:  ([Yuma Consensus: Validator emissions](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus#validator-emissions)).
142:
143:## Reader Boundary
144:
145:Subnet 77 Liquidity should not be read as generic Bittensor subnet documentation, a yield-farming
146:product, or investment advice. It names one subnet's liquidity-incentive market — token-holder votes
147:steer incentives and miners compete by supplying liquidity to selected decentralized-exchange pools
148:— on netuid 77
149:([Understanding Subnets](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/subnets/understanding-subnets),
150:[Glossary: Netuid](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/resources/glossary#netuid)).
151:
152:A miner's reward reflects how useful its supplied liquidity is to the pools favored by the subnet's
153:voting process, not any return, fee, or price outcome from providing that liquidity
154:([Subnet 77 on TaoStats](https://taostats.io/subnets/77)).

## Voting and Weighting

The subnet introduces a voting layer so token holders can express which pools should receive
attention. Those votes guide the system toward pools that the community wants to support, instead of
leaving liquidity incentives fixed by a single static list.

Validators translate the voting and liquidity information into subnet weights. At a high level, they
look at which pools have been selected, how liquidity is distributed across those pools, and which
miners are responsible for that liquidity. Those weights then flow through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) to determine how rewards are
allocated on the subnet.

## Vote and Liquidity Mapping Context

The
[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md)
describes three separate pieces that have to line up before miner rewards make sense: token-holder
votes, liquidity positions, and Bittensor miner identities. Token holders vote on which pools should
receive weight, miners provide liquidity to those pools, and validators fetch calculated weights
that connect the two.

That mapping is important because the useful work happens outside the Bittensor chain. A miner's
liquidity position is associated with an EVM address, while subnet rewards are assigned to a
Bittensor hotkey. The README describes a registration step that links those identities, giving the
weighting system a way to credit the miner responsible for a liquidity position.

The same source also describes pool voting as weighted rather than purely one-person-one-vote. Votes
express which liquidity pools token holders want to support, while miner positions determine who is
actually supplying liquidity inside those selected pools. For readers, the subnet is therefore not
rewarding liquidity in isolation and not rewarding votes alone; it is combining both signals into a
liquidity-incentive market.

That combination also keeps the article's DeFi framing precise. Pool demand can change as token
holders update preferences, while miner credit depends on whether capital is actually present in the
relevant pools. The reward target is useful, attributable liquidity for pools the mechanism has
selected.

This helps explain why validators are described as a measurement layer. Their role is not to invent
the preferred pools manually. They follow the calculated weight result from the vote and liquidity
system, then submit subnet weights so emissions track the pools and miner positions the mechanism is
meant to incentivize.

References:
[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
[Subnet 77 repository](https://github.com/creativebuilds/sn77)

## Miner and Validator Roles

Miners are liquidity providers. Their job is to place capital where the subnet is directing
liquidity demand, then keep enough useful liquidity in those pools to remain competitive.

Validators are the measurement and weighting layer. They follow the subnet's voting result, observe
the liquidity supplied by miners, and set weights that flow through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) into emissions each tempo, so
rewards track the pools and positions the subnet is meant to incentivize. Operators should treat the
Subnet 77 repository as the source of truth for current implementation details.

## On-Chain Identity

Live SN77 subnet data is available on [TaoStats](https://taostats.io/subnets/77), which identifies
Subnet 77 as Liquidity. The live Finney identity for netuid 77 registers the subnet name as
Liquidity, with the description "Supply liquidity on external chains via uniswap, incentivize any
project." The registered project website is [sn77.xyz](https://sn77.xyz) and the GitHub repository
is [creativebuilds/sn77](https://github.com/creativebuilds/sn77), whose README describes the voting,
liquidity, and validator-weighting model used by the subnet.

Validators translate the voting and liquidity information into subnet weights. At a high level, they
look at which pools have been selected, how liquidity is distributed across those pools, and which
miners are responsible for that liquidity. Those weights then flow through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) to determine how rewards are
allocated on the subnet.

## Identity Mapping Boundary

The
[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md)
describes SN77 as measuring liquidity that miners place in external liquidity pools. Those
[[Liquidity Positions]] live on external DEX infrastructure, so the observable activity is tied to
an EVM address or a pool position rather than to a Bittensor account.

Reward attribution therefore depends on an identity bridge. Subnet rewards are paid to Bittensor
hotkeys, but the liquidity being measured exists outside the chain. The README describes a
registration step that connects a miner's EVM address or liquidity position back to that miner's
Bittensor hotkey, so validators can credit the right participant before weights flow into
[[Yuma Consensus]].

## Pool Selection Boundary

The same source also makes clear that identity mapping alone is not enough. Token holders vote on
which pools should matter, miners supply liquidity to those selected pools, and validators combine
both signals when setting weights.

That creates a second boundary: the subnet must decide which external pools count before it can
decide which registered miner should be rewarded. In practice, SN77 maps external pool activity back
to a hotkey only after the voting process has identified the pools that should receive incentive
weight. That is how outside liquidity provision becomes Bittensor rewards instead of staying as
unattributed DeFi activity.

References:
[Subnet 77 README source](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/creativebuilds/sn77/master/README.md),
[Subnet 77 repository](https://github.com/creativebuilds/sn77)

## Miner and Validator Roles

Miners are liquidity providers. Their job is to place capital where the subnet is directing
liquidity demand, then keep enough useful liquidity in those pools to remain competitive.

Validators are the measurement and weighting layer. They follow the subnet's voting result, observe
the liquidity supplied by miners, and set weights that flow through
[Yuma Consensus](https://docs.learnbittensor.org/learn/yuma-consensus) into emissions each tempo, so
rewards track the pools and positions the subnet is meant to incentivize. Operators should treat the
Subnet 77 repository as the source of truth for current implementation details.
